Functional Capacity · Calibration Spec

Defines the first-step scaffold for the three-axis functional capacity teaching tool. Separates what currently holds from what still needs empirical calibration.

Purpose

IN BRIEF This spec does not claim the current prototype is calibrated. It defines the first step: keep the three-axis logic, make the functional capacity bands concrete, and use the current curve only as a falsifiable sketch. The curve should be replaced by empirical trajectory templates before any validation claim.

The prototype's architecture remains useful: three orthogonal axes, geometry versus render split, and real-world functional capacity bands. The current single-formula curve is not held fixed. It is a temporary scaffold that should be tested against reality and replaced when it fails.

Defined Terms

This document uses the following terms with specific meanings. They are referenced throughout the body without re-definition.

Risk · residualized
10-year probability of a major adverse cardiovascular event, residualized against the reserve composite. The portion of risk not already explained by capacity.
Reserve · capacity
Multi-domain functional capacity composite: cardiorespiratory, musculoskeletal, skeletal, cognitive, balance, pulmonary. Age- and sex-normalized to a 0 to 100 percentile.
Lifestyle · felt
Composite of validated patient-reported outcomes covering felt vitality dimensions that are non-physical and non-capacity. Sleep, mood, stress, connection, purpose, drive, libido, pain, fatigue.
Vitality (felt)
The single-construct outcome that the line render encodes. v1 uses SF-36 Vitality (4 items, 0 to 100). Distinct from the Lifestyle composite, which is one of the two predictors of Vitality.
Functional Capacity
The curve's y-axis. Equal-weighted composite of physical function, cognitive function, social participation, and independence at age A. Bounded 0 to 100. Death = 0.
Render mix
The 0 to 1 quantity that drives line color, width, and glow. Currently 0.5·Reserve + 0.5·Lifestyle. Calibrated form: regression coefficients from the Vitality fit.
Age decay
The age-driven attenuation of the render mix. Currently a logistic with floor, slope, and center modulated by reserve. Calibrated form: parameters fit from longitudinal vitality trajectories.
Avg American
The dashed reference curve and preset. The median U.S. adult's position on each axis, computed from public-health surveillance data.

Note on the Functional Capacity defined term above. The 4-domain equal-weighted composite (PROMIS PF, MoCA, PROMIS Ability to Participate, Lawton+Katz) is superseded by the y-axis decision recorded in the next section. The composite was the prior assumption. It does not survive scrutiny, for reasons given below. The defined term will be rewritten in the next spec uprev. Sections §4 and §5 still reference the old composite and will be updated in the same uprev.

Y-axis: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The curve's y-axis is VO₂max in mL/kg/min, percentile-normed for age and sex against the FRIEND registry. This was selected after explicit comparison of nine candidate scales. It is the only single coherent measurement that has resolution at both the upper end of the curve (differentiating elite-fit from recreationally-fit from sedentary healthy adults aged 30 to 70) and the lower end (frailty, ADL dependence, late-life decline), while also having published lifespan trajectory data and physician recognition. The cost of this choice is that cognitive decline is not represented on this axis. That cost is accepted and disclosed.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis is the most propagating decision in the tool. Every band, every lever calibration, every empirical trajectory template, and every claim the tool makes about felt experience depends on what the y-axis measures. Choosing it casually, or as a composite assembled from convenience, weakens every downstream claim. The prior version of this spec defined the y-axis as a 4-domain equal-weighted composite of PROMIS PF, cognition, social participation, and independence. That composite is the failure mode this section corrects.

Why the prior 4-domain composite fails

  1. Frankenstein construction. The four instruments measure incommensurable things on incommensurable scales. Equal weighting is arbitrary and not derived from any underlying model of capacity.
  2. Severe ceiling effect at the top. PROMIS PF v1.2/v2.0 ceilings around T-score 60 to 65 even with the CAT (Hung 2014, Schalet 2016). Lawton IADL and Katz ADL ceiling at maximum independence. Healthy adults aged 30 to 60 all pile near the top of the composite, erasing exactly the granularity the user cares about.
  3. No published lifespan trajectory. No cohort has measured this specific composite from young-adult peak through old-age decline. Any trajectory built on it would be an interpolation across instruments, not an empirical curve.
  4. Not physician-legible as a single number. A clinician cannot translate "your composite is 67" into a clinical assessment, because there is no clinic where this composite is measured.

Constraints the y-axis must satisfy

  1. Single coherent measurement, not a composite of unrelated instruments.
  2. Top-end resolution. Must distinguish elite-fit from recreationally-fit from sedentary healthy adults aged 30 to 70.
  3. Bottom-end resolution. Must map cleanly to frailty, ADL dependence, and care-need thresholds.
  4. Lifespan trajectory data. Published longitudinal data from age 20 to 90+, ideally from a single cohort.
  5. Physician-legible. A clinician should recognize the number and the bands without explanation.
  6. Honest about what it does not measure. The scale should not pretend to capture dimensions it cannot.

The irreducible tradeoff in the field

The literature has no instrument that satisfies all six constraints. Top-end granularity and bottom-end clinical meaning are inversely correlated across every validated scale. Anything calibrated to detect ADL loss (Intrinsic Capacity, SPPB, 6MWD, PROMIS PF, Healthy Aging Index, Frailty Index) ceilings before age 50 in healthy adults. Anything that resolves elite fitness (VO₂max, power output, masters-athlete percentiles) maps imperfectly onto disability cutoffs. This gap is the field's gap, not this tool's. The choice is which side of the tradeoff to honor.

Why VO₂max wins

  1. Top-end resolution. Elite endurance 70 to 85 mL/kg/min, recreationally fit 40 to 50, sedentary 25 to 35, pre-frail 20, ADL-threshold 15, bedbound <10. Continuous, no ceiling effect across the range that matters.
  2. Bottom-end resolution. VO₂max <17.5 (men) and <15.5 (women) maps to functional dependence (Paterson and Warburton 2010). The same number that distinguishes recreational riders from competitive ones distinguishes "lives alone safely" from "needs daily help."
  3. Lifespan trajectory data. Fleg et al. (BLSA, Circulation 2005) published longitudinal VO₂max trajectories from age 20 to 90+ with accelerating decline (~3 to 6%/decade in 20s, >20%/decade after 70). Kaminsky et al. (Mayo Clin Proc 2015, 2022) published the FRIEND registry percentile norms.
  4. Physician recognition. AHA 2016 statement (Ross et al., Circulation) designated cardiorespiratory fitness as a clinical vital sign. Mandsager 2018 (JAMA Netw Open) showed CRF predicts all-cause mortality more strongly than smoking, hypertension, or diabetes. Used in cardio rehab, surgical risk stratification, and longevity medicine practice.
  5. Single coherent measurement. Not a composite. One physiological quantity, measurable directly via CPET, estimable via submaximal protocols.
  6. Lever responsiveness is well-characterized. Training raises VO₂max ~15 to 25%. Sedentary lifestyle accelerates decline ~2x. Smoking, untreated disease, and chronic inflammation drop it. The levers in this tool can be calibrated against published intervention literature.

What this choice explicitly does not measure

Cognitive decline is not represented on this axis. A person with preserved cardiorespiratory fitness and progressive dementia will appear "fine" on this curve. Sensory loss (vision, hearing) is similarly not captured. Specific neuromuscular power (which declines faster than VO₂max in late life, Reid and Fielding 2012) is approximated but not directly measured.

The tool will disclose this on the visible surface. The framing: "This shows the cardiorespiratory and physical capability curve. Cognitive trajectory is a separate axis the tool does not render." A composite that pretended to capture cognition would have been the worse choice, because it would have been the Frankenstein this section rejects.

Bands

Bands are capability-verb statements anchored to VO₂max ranges. Numbers appear as small left-axis ticks. Bands are the semantic ground in the chart background. The boundaries are continuous gradients, not hard cuts, because VO₂max is continuous.

Visual treatment of the axis

  1. VO₂max linear on the y-axis. The cliff bands at the bottom will look narrow because the cliff is short and steep. That visual narrowness is the truth.
  2. Single axis, not two-sided. Numbers as ticks, bands as zones. Two-sided axes split attention.
  3. Bands as gradient zones in the background, not stacked colored bars with sharp lines. The viewer should feel the transition, not see a step.
  4. Band names render as floating text inside each zone. Capability-verb language, not sport-codes.

Open questions deferred to implementation

  1. Whether to also display sex-specific bands or absolute VO₂max only. Absolute is simpler and clinically standard; percentile-normed is more honest about relative position. Likely answer: absolute on the axis, percentile in the readout.
  2. Whether the cognitive-decline disclaimer lives as a permanent caption, a footnote, or a one-time onboarding card. Argues for caption, since the disclosure must travel with the chart.
  3. How to handle the very-old tail (90+) where measurement protocols become unreliable. Likely answer: render dashed beyond age 90 to signal increasing uncertainty.
  4. Whether avg American reference uses FRIEND 50th percentile by age, NHANES estimated VO₂max, or BLSA cohort means. Likely answer: FRIEND, because it is the most current and largest U.S. reference dataset.

Lever architecture: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The three levers that shape the curve are Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, and Residual Risk. Conditioning is a state slider (elite to deconditioned) whose position implies sustained behavior; it sets curve altitude and decline slope. Lifestyle Optimization covers non-training inputs that support vitality (sleep, nutrition, stress and recovery, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence) and has two mechanisms: a small effect on decline slope, and a large effect on the vitality color render of the line. Residual Risk is the irreducible event-propensity bucket (genetics, prior disease, environmental exposure) and introduces step-downs from discrete events. The architecture replaces the prior Risk / Reserve / Lifestyle naming, which had unresolved overlap with the new VO₂max y-axis.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis decision propagates directly into how levers are defined. Once the y-axis is VO₂max, the prior Reserve lever (an 8-component capacity composite that included cardiorespiratory fitness) collapses into the y-axis itself: the lever and the chart axis would be measuring the same construct. The prior Risk definition (residualized against capacity) becomes circular: residualizing against the chart itself. The prior Lifestyle definition included training behavior, which is now redundant with Conditioning. The lever set must be redrawn so each slider has a distinct mechanism and no double-counting.

Why the prior Risk / Reserve / Lifestyle framing fails under the new y-axis

  1. Reserve overlapped the y-axis. The cardiorespiratory component of Reserve is VO₂max, which is now the y-axis. The lever and the dependent variable measured the same thing.
  2. Risk residualization became circular. "Risk not explained by capacity" requires capacity to be a separate construct. When capacity is the chart, the residualization has nothing clean to residualize against.
  3. Lifestyle conflated physical and non-physical inputs. Sleep and training behavior were in the same bucket despite operating through different mechanisms with different evidence bases.

The three levers, redrawn

Conditioning · state slider

Range: Elite → Well-conditioned → Average → Deconditioned.

What it represents: The user's current physical conditioning state, with the implicit assumption that the slider position reflects sustained behavior. Setting the slider to Elite means the person lives in a way that produces and maintains elite conditioning. Setting it to Deconditioned means sustained low activity.

Mechanism on the curve: Sets baseline VO₂max anchor and decline slope. This is the dominant lever. Elite-conditioned adults sit ~25 mL/kg/min above sedentary peers at age 50 and decline ~50% slower (BLSA, masters athletes literature).

Why a state, not a behavior: Training is the input; conditioning is the result. Only the result drives the curve. A separate training slider would double-count.

Why this is not the same as the y-axis: The slider is set once, by the user, as a self-assessment. The y-axis is the rendered VO₂max trajectory at every age. The slider says "this is who I am"; the curve says "this is how that trajectory unfolds."

Lifestyle Optimization · non-training inputs that support vitality

Range: Highly Optimized → Maintained → Neglected.

What it represents: Non-training behavioral and medical inputs that support vitality. Sleep behavior, nutrition, stress and recovery load, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence. Does not include training behavior — that lives in Conditioning.

Why "Optimization" rather than "Lifestyle": The lever covers both passive behaviors (sleep, social) and active medical layers (hormone panels, metabolic optimization, supplementation). "Lifestyle" alone reads as passive description. "Optimization" reads as deliberate stance, which is what the slider position represents — how much effort someone puts into vitality-supporting inputs across both behavioral and medical domains.

Why hormone management lives here: Hormone optimization (testosterone, estrogen, thyroid, metabolic panels) belongs in the same bucket as other vitality-supporting inputs because it operates through the same mechanism: supporting felt energy, mood, recovery, drive, and connection. It is not the only medical optimization input, but it is named in the lever definition because it is a meaningful and increasingly common practice in the longevity-medicine space.

Mechanism on the curve, dual:

  1. Small effect on slope. Sleep deprivation, chronic stress, and social isolation accelerate VO₂max decline; the inverse buffers it. Effect size is real but modest and weakly calibrated against VO₂max trajectory specifically. ±5 to 10% slope modulation is the defensible range.
  2. Large effect on vitality color render. The same inputs have well-documented effects on felt vitality (SF-36 Vitality, energy, mood, drive). This is the lever's primary mechanism. See the Vitality decision log section for full treatment.

Why this lever still matters even though its slope effect is small: Vitality is a first-class outcome the tool exists to teach. A patient at VO₂max 35 can feel vital or depleted based on this lever, and that difference matters more in lived experience than a 5% slope change. Lifestyle Optimization is also the lever a Deconditioned person can move first, before they can credibly move Conditioning.

Residual Risk · irreducible event propensity

Range: Low → Average → High.

What it represents: Event-propensity factors not captured by Conditioning or Lifestyle. Includes age, sex, family history, prior disease, imaging findings (CAC, plaque, BMD, liver fat), inherited variants, environmental exposures, medication and treatment history, stochastic baseline.

Mechanism on the curve: Introduces discrete step-downs from events (MI, stroke, heart failure, hip fracture, treatment cardiotoxicity). Higher Residual Risk means earlier and more severe events. The event drops are visible in the curve as a different visual signature than Conditioning's smooth slope or Lifestyle's slope modulation.

Why "Residual": Conditioning and Lifestyle already capture much of what drives event risk in the population. Residual Risk is what is left after those two are accounted for: the part of risk you cannot move with behavior.

Lever weight: not equal

Conditioning is the dominant lever. It sets both baseline and slope. Lifestyle modulates slope at the margins. Residual Risk introduces events but does not change the underlying trajectory shape between events. The viewer will see Conditioning move the curve more than the other two. This is correct and reflects the literature. The tool should not frame the three levers as equal; it should frame them as three distinct mechanisms with different magnitudes.

Independence: pedagogical, not physiological

In nature the levers are correlated. Conditioning lowers Risk. Lifestyle (sleep, stress) affects training capacity which affects Conditioning. An MI drops Conditioning. We declare independence by construction for the teaching tool: each slider modifies exactly one mechanism on the curve, and we disclose the cross-talk explicitly in a "what this tool simplifies" panel. The independence claim is pedagogical, not physiological. The tool teaches the structure of the levers; it does not pretend they are orthogonal in life.

What this architecture gives the viewer

Three sliders, three visually distinct effects on the curve. Conditioning shifts altitude and tilts the slope. Lifestyle bends the slope subtly. Residual Risk drops step-downs onto the trajectory. A patient sees what each lever does on its own; a provider sees how to weight a care plan across the three. This is the teaching payload the tool exists to deliver.

Open questions deferred to modeling decisions

  1. Stochastic events vs deterministic representative trajectory. (Likely: deterministic, modal-age events for the given Residual Risk profile.)
  2. Event scope. (Likely: MI, stroke, HF, hip fracture, treatment cardiotoxicity. Skip dementia, off-axis by spec.)
  3. Recovery dynamics parameterization. (Likely: per-event acute drop magnitude and asymptotic recovery fraction; fixed time constant per event class.)
  4. Whether Conditioning and Lifestyle independence holds at the time-scale of the teaching tool, given that Lifestyle inputs feed Conditioning over years. (Likely: declared orthogonal by construction; cross-talk disclosed.)
  5. Whether the indirect path (Conditioning → Risk reduction) is modeled or omitted. (Likely: modeled, because omitting it understates Conditioning's true effect.)

Sections superseded by this decision

§1 (Risk · residualized), §2 (Reserve · capacity), and §3 (Lifestyle · felt) reflect the prior framing and will be rewritten in the next spec uprev to match the architecture above. They are retained in this version as the historical record of what this section replaces.

Vitality color render: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The chart renders two layers, not one. The line's position is the VO₂max trajectory (capacity over time). The line's color is felt vitality at each point of the trajectory. Vitality is operationalized as a continuous index drawing from validated patient-reported outcome instruments covering energy, mood, drive, connection, and pain. Conditioning contributes meaningfully to vitality because cardiovascular fitness genuinely supports felt energy, but Lifestyle Optimization is the dominant input. Vitality fades with age regardless of conditioning, because lived experience does. The render exists because capacity and vitality are genuinely different outcomes and a single curve cannot show both.

Why this decision matters

Capacity (what your body can do) and vitality (how living that life feels) are not the same. A 60-year-old at VO₂max 35 can feel alive or depleted depending on sleep, mood, hormones, pain, and connection. A 30-year-old at VO₂max 50 can be high-capacity and exhausted. The teaching tool exists in part to communicate that distinction. Without a vitality render, the tool would teach only physical capability and would miss the lived-experience layer that physicians and patients actually care about.

The vitality render is also what gives Lifestyle Optimization its primary mechanism. The slope effect of Lifestyle Optimization on VO₂max trajectory is real but modest and weakly calibrated. The color effect is large and well-supported. Without the vitality render, Lifestyle Optimization would be a weak lever. With it, the lever has a primary visual signature that reflects its real-world importance.

What vitality is, and what it is not

Vitality is: the felt experience of being alive at a given physical capacity. Energy, mood, drive, connection, sense of purpose, freedom from pain and fatigue, libido, presence. Downstream of physiology and behavior, but a first-class outcome in its own right.

Vitality is not: physical capability, cognitive function, or risk of disease. It is not measured by VO₂max, by ADL count, by frailty index, or by mortality probability. A person can have high vitality at any physical capacity, and low vitality at any physical capacity. The independence is not perfect, but it is real.

Operationalization

Vitality is a continuous 0 to 100 index drawing from validated patient-reported outcome dimensions. v1 anchors the construct to SF-36 Vitality (4 items: feel full of life, have energy, feel worn out, feel tired) for its single-construct definition and longitudinal data history. v2 broadens to a multi-instrument composite covering vitality dimensions not captured by SF-36 alone: PROMIS Emotional Distress (mood), PROMIS Social Health (connection), brief sexual function (drive), pain interference, and purpose-in-life. The v2 composite is acknowledged as composite construction, but is justified here because vitality is genuinely multidimensional in lived experience — collapsing to one instrument would underrepresent it.

Why a composite is acceptable here when a composite was rejected for the y-axis: The y-axis decision required a single coherent measurement because the curve is the dependent variable and any composite would have inherited the Frankenstein problem (incommensurable scales, arbitrary weights, no published trajectory data). Vitality as render is a secondary visual layer informed by a composite is acceptable because the render does not need a published lifespan trajectory. It needs to encode the directional signal of felt experience at each point of the curve. Different evidence requirement, different acceptable construction.

Inputs to vitality

Vitality at age A is a function of three inputs:

  1. Lifestyle Optimization — dominant input. Sleep, nutrition, stress and recovery, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence all directly drive felt vitality. This is where the lever has its primary teaching signal.
  2. Conditioning — meaningful contribution. Cardiovascular fitness genuinely supports felt energy and recovery. A Highly Optimized but Deconditioned person will not feel as vital as a Highly Optimized and Well-conditioned person, because the body itself contributes to felt aliveness. The render mix should reflect this.
  3. Age — vitality fades with age regardless of the other two inputs, because lived experience does. Even at peak optimization and elite conditioning, vitality at age 85 is lower than at age 35. The age decay is gentle for high-optimization, high-conditioning profiles and steeper for low ones.

Residual Risk does not directly drive vitality between events. Residual Risk drives event step-downs in the capacity curve; events themselves cause acute vitality drops via pain, recovery burden, and psychological impact. The vitality render handles this by dropping color saturation and brightness around event windows, recovering as the curve recovers.

Render mix, v1 form

The render mix is a 0 to 1 quantity that drives line color, brightness, saturation, and weight. v1 form:

render_mix(age) = 0.65 · Lifestyle_Optimization 
                + 0.35 · Conditioning_contribution
                − age_decay(age, Lifestyle, Conditioning)
                − event_acute_drop(age, Risk)

The 0.65 / 0.35 weight reflects that Lifestyle Optimization is the dominant input but Conditioning contributes meaningfully. The age decay term ensures vitality fades across a lifetime even at peak inputs. The event acute drop term ensures events visibly tank vitality at the moment of impact and during recovery.

v2 calibration replaces the 0.65 / 0.35 weights with regression coefficients fit against the vitality composite outcome in UK Biobank cross-sectional and HRS longitudinal data. v1 weights are clinical-prior estimates.

Visual treatment

  1. Color ramp: red (depleted) through neutral through green (vital). Red-green ramp tested for color-blind safety; alternative is dark-purple to bright-amber if red-green is rejected.
  2. Brightness and saturation co-vary with the render mix. High vitality is bright and saturated; low vitality is muted and desaturated.
  3. Line weight scales subtly with vitality. High vitality reads as a confident, full line; low vitality reads as a thinner, less-present line.
  4. No fill underneath the curve. The line itself carries the render. Filled area under the curve would compete visually with the band zones in the chart background.
  5. Per-segment rendering. The curve is sampled at fine intervals (every 0.5 years) and each segment is colored by the vitality at that age. The viewer sees a gradient along the curve, not a monochrome line.

Why Conditioning contributes to vitality, not just Lifestyle Optimization

The prior version of this model attempted to make Lifestyle the sole render input and treat Conditioning as render-neutral. That construction is not honest. Cardiovascular fitness contributes to felt energy in a way the longevity-medicine literature consistently documents. A Highly Optimized but Deconditioned person feels less vital than a Highly Optimized and Well-conditioned person, holding everything else constant. The render mix therefore must include Conditioning. The 0.65 / 0.35 split is the v1 estimate; the precise weight is a v2 calibration target.

The cross-talk this creates with the lever architecture

Conditioning now has two effects: capacity (its primary role) and vitality (its secondary role). This means moving the Conditioning slider visibly changes both the line position and the line color. That is correct and reflects reality. It does mean Conditioning is even more dominant in the model than the lever architecture section already acknowledged. The "three sliders, three independent effects" frame becomes "three sliders, four to five distinct visible effects." The teaching frame still holds — the viewer sees what each lever does — but the model is honest that Conditioning's reach is larger than a single slider's worth of effect.

Open questions deferred to implementation

  1. Whether v1 ships with SF-36 Vitality as the sole anchor, or with the broader v2 composite from the start. Argues for SF-36 in v1, composite as a v2 target documented in the spec.
  2. Whether age decay parameters differ by sex. Likely yes, because vitality trajectories differ; deferred to calibration.
  3. Whether the event-acute-drop term recovers fully or leaves a residual vitality penalty after event recovery. Likely partial residual, calibrated to literature on post-event quality of life.
  4. Whether the color ramp has a fixed scale (e.g., 0 to 100 vitality maps to red-to-green absolutely) or a relative scale (the user's own range across slider settings). Likely fixed, because relative would let any setting look bright.

Modeling decisions: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF Levers are mutually exclusive on the curve by construction; physiological cross-talk is disclosed in a panel rather than modeled. Events are deterministic and fire at modal ages for the given Residual Risk profile. Event scope covers all five Meridian risk domains (cardiovascular, CKD, metabolic, cancer, neurodegenerative) with domain-specific representation: cardiovascular as discrete events, CKD as slope modifier with dialysis step-down, metabolic syndrome as risk-multiplier (not events), cancer as treatment-burden events, neurodegenerative as a dashed-curve visual signature signaling the tool's limit. Residual Risk remains a single aggregate slider; per-domain risk profiles are simplified into a single dial for the teaching frame.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis and lever architecture sections fixed what the tool measures and which levers shape it. This section fixes how the levers map to the curve mechanically and which events appear. These are the last decisions before code. Each one propagates: the orthogonality choice changes how cross-talk is communicated; the event scope changes the curve's visual signature; the event-firing model changes whether the curve is reproducible across renders. Locking these here means implementation does not invent answers under deadline.

Decision 1 · Mutual exclusivity by construction

Choice: Each lever has exactly one mechanism on the curve. Physiological cross-talk is disclosed in a panel, not modeled.

Reasoning: The teaching payload of the tool is the structure of the levers. A patient or provider should be able to move one slider and see one effect. Modeling cross-talk muddies the signal: moving Conditioning would change the curve through three pathways (capacity, vitality, event probability) and the viewer cannot decompose what they are seeing. The pedagogical orthogonality principle from the lever architecture section applies here: levers are pedagogically independent, not physiologically independent. The cross-talk disclosure panel preserves honesty without sacrificing teaching clarity.

What this overrides: The earlier recommendation to model the indirect Conditioning → Risk path. Under mutual exclusivity, Conditioning's protective effect on cardiovascular events is disclosed in the cross-talk panel rather than baked into event probability. The viewer learns the protective relationship from the disclosure, not from the slider behavior.

Cross-talk disclosure panel content:

  • Conditioning lowers cardiovascular and metabolic event risk in reality. The tool does not model this; it teaches Conditioning and Residual Risk as separate levers.
  • Lifestyle Optimization (sleep, nutrition, stress) affects cardiovascular and metabolic event risk in reality. The tool does not model this; it teaches the levers separately.
  • Cardiovascular events drop Conditioning in reality. The tool does not adjust Conditioning slider position when events fire; it shows the event step-down on the curve and lets Conditioning remain at its set position.
  • Lifestyle Optimization inputs (sleep, stress) affect training capacity over years and therefore Conditioning. The tool treats them as orthogonal at the rendering moment.

Decision 2 · Deterministic events at modal ages

Choice: Events are deterministic. For a given Residual Risk profile, the tool draws events at their modal ages. The curve is reproducible across renders.

Reasoning: A teaching tool that re-rolls events on every slider change is unusable. The viewer cannot tell if a curve change is due to slider movement or stochastic resampling. Deterministic events with a clear "this is one representative path" caption gives the viewer a stable anchor while preserving honesty about variance.

Decision 3 · Event scope, by Meridian risk domain

Choice: All five Meridian risk domains appear, with domain-specific representation honest to each domain's mechanism.

Cardiovascular · discrete events

MI, stroke, heart failure onset. Strong calibration: cardiac rehab literature provides VO₂max pre/post curves. MI: ~20–30% acute drop, partial recovery over 6–12 months. Stroke: highly variable (10–40% drop), partial recovery over 12–24 months. Heart failure: progressive decline interleaved with acute decompensations. The cardiovascular domain is the cleanest fit for the discrete-event-on-curve model.

Chronic kidney disease · slope modifier with dialysis step-down

CKD does not fit the discrete-event model. eGFR descent through stages is gradual, not a moment. CKD also lowers VO₂max independently through uremic myopathy, anemia, and fluid status. Representation: a slope modifier proportional to CKD stage that progressively reduces VO₂max trajectory, with a step-down event at dialysis initiation (~30% acute drop on top of accumulated slope effect). For Average Residual Risk profiles CKD progresses to stage 3–4 in late life without dialysis; for High profiles dialysis fires at modal age 76. Disclosure: the curve segment showing CKD effect is annotated as "CKD-driven slope" so the viewer can distinguish it from age-driven decline.

Metabolic syndrome · risk-multiplier, not events

Metabolic syndrome is a state, not a thing that happens. It elevates cardiovascular event probability, accelerates kidney decline, and increases cancer incidence. Modeling metabolic syndrome as discrete events would misrepresent its mechanism. Instead, it is folded into Residual Risk as a multiplier: higher metabolic burden raises event frequency across CV, kidney, and cancer domains. The viewer does not see "metabolic events" on the curve. The viewer sees CV, kidney, and cancer events firing earlier and more often when Residual Risk is higher because metabolic burden contributes to that aggregate.

Why not give metabolic its own visual signature: the temptation is to render metabolic syndrome as a smooth slope penalty so the viewer can see "metabolic burden" on the curve. This was rejected because metabolic syndrome's effect on VO₂max is largely indirect (through cardiac, kidney, and treatment-burden pathways already represented). A direct slope penalty would double-count those pathways.

Cancer · treatment-burden events

Cancer itself does not drop VO₂max acutely in most cases. Treatment is what drives the capacity loss: chemo cardiotoxicity, radiation pulmonary effects, surgical recovery, deconditioning during treatment. Survivors often carry 10–30% permanent VO₂max reductions. Representation: a "cancer treatment burden" event class with acute drop (~25%) during the treatment window and partial recovery to a residual penalty (~10–15% permanent). The event is labeled "cancer treatment" rather than "cancer," which is honest about the mechanism. Disclosure: caption notes that the drop is treatment-driven, and that some cancers and treatments have larger or smaller effects than the modal trajectory.

Neurodegenerative · dashed-curve visual signature

Neurodegenerative disease (dementia, Parkinson's) presents the hardest case. The y-axis decision explicitly excluded cognitive decline. But excluding neurodegenerative entirely understates a domain that matters profoundly to lived outcomes. Three options were considered:

  1. Include as late-life motor decline only. Faithful to the y-axis but understates the real impact.
  2. Render as a distinct visual signature. A dashed curve segment beginning at neurodegenerative onset, signaling "this curve no longer represents the full trajectory; cognitive and ADL decline are happening on a separate axis the tool does not render."
  3. Exclude entirely; flag in disclosure. Honest about the tool's limit.

Choice: option 2. Reasoning: option 1 lies by partial inclusion. Option 3 walks away from a domain Meridian covers. Option 2 respects the y-axis decision while not pretending the domain does not exist. The dashed segment becomes its own teaching moment — the tool visibly reaches its limit, and that limit is a feature, not a defect. A physician sees the dashed segment and immediately understands what the tool is and is not claiming. Modal age for neurodegenerative onset (where the curve transitions from solid to dashed): age 79 for Average Risk, age 73 for High Risk; not rendered for Low Risk.

Decision 4 · Recovery dynamics parameterization

Choice: Each event class is parameterized by three numbers: acute drop magnitude (% of pre-event VO₂max), recovery time constant (months to plateau), and asymptotic recovery fraction (% of acute drop recovered at plateau).

Reasoning: Three parameters per event class is the minimum required to represent the shape of recovery (drop, return-to-asymptote, residual penalty) without overfitting. Calibration targets exist in the literature for each event class:

Decision 5 · Conditioning ↔ Lifestyle Optimization independence

Choice: Declared orthogonal by construction. Cross-talk disclosed in the panel covered by Decision 1.

Reasoning: Already covered above. The two levers are physiologically coupled (sleep affects training capacity, training affects sleep quality, hormone optimization affects both, etc.). The teaching tool benefits from independence. The disclosure handles the honesty cost.

Architectural decision · Single Residual Risk slider

Choice: Residual Risk remains a single aggregate slider. Per-domain risks (CV, kidney, cancer, neurodegenerative) are not exposed as separate sliders.

Reasoning: Fragmenting the slider into four or five domain-specific sliders is more physiologically honest — a real person can have high CV risk and low cancer risk — but it breaks the three-lever architecture and turns the tool into a personalized risk calculator. The tool's purpose is teaching, not personalized prediction. The single slider sets aggregate residual risk; under the hood each domain's events fire at the modal ages tabulated in Decision 2. Disclosure: "Real risk profiles are domain-specific. The slider represents aggregate residual risk for teaching purposes."

What this is not: a claim that a real person's CV and cancer risks are correlated at this level. They are not, in many cases. The simplification is acknowledged as a teaching simplification, not a clinical claim.

What is now locked, and what is still open

Locked: y-axis (VO₂max FRIEND-normed), lever architecture (Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, Residual Risk), vitality color render, mutual exclusivity, deterministic events, event scope across five domains, recovery parameterization, single Residual Risk slider.

Still open (deferred to implementation): precise modal-age calibration values, recovery parameter tuning, sex-specific trajectories, very-old tail uncertainty rendering, cross-talk panel UI placement and copy, color ramp accessibility audit, empirical reference trajectory library construction.

1 · Risk · residualized

IN BRIEF Risk is operationalized as 10-year MACE probability from the AHA PREVENT model, residualized against the reserve composite to remove the variance reserve already explains. The slider value is the residual ranked against the NHANES adult distribution. v1 is cardiovascular only; cancer, dementia, and fracture are deferred to v2. Adults 30 to 79 to stay in PREVENT validity range.

Construct

10-year probability of MI, stroke, cardiovascular death, or hospitalized heart failure.

Base model

PREVENT (Predicting Risk of CVD EVENTs), AHA 2024. Inputs: age, sex, total cholesterol, HDL-C, SBP, antihypertensive use, current smoking, diabetes, eGFR, BMI, statin use. Optional: UACR, A1c, social deprivation index.

Residualization procedure

1. PREVENT_pct = percentile(PREVENT 10yr CVD, NHANES adults) 2. predicted = β₀ + β₁ · Reserve_pct [linear regression on NHANES] 3. residual = PREVENT_pct − predicted 4. Risk_slider = percentile(residual, residual_distribution)

Slider mapping

  • 0 = lowest 1st percentile residualized risk after accounting for reserve
  • 100 = highest 99th percentile residualized risk
  • Avg American = computed from NHANES (replaces eyeballed 65)

v1 simplifications

  • Linear residualization. v2 may use GAM or quantile regression if nonlinearity is meaningful.
  • Single 10-year time horizon.
  • Cardiovascular events only; multi-domain risk deferred.
  • Adults 30 to 79; pediatric and very-elderly out of scope.

2 · Reserve · capacity

IN BRIEF Reserve is an eight-component composite covering the major domains of functional capacity. Each component is normalized to age- and sex-adjusted percentile against NHANES (US) or HUNT4 (Norway, more complete activity data), then weighted and summed to a 0 to 100 score. v1 weights are clinical-prior; v2 derives them by regressing all-cause mortality against the components in UK Biobank. Avg American is computed by composing each NHANES component median.

Normalization

For each component i: z_i = (raw_i − μ(age, sex)) / σ(age, sex) [reference: NHANES or HUNT4] p_i = Φ(z_i) · 100 Reserve_pct = Σ wᵢ · pᵢ

Slider mapping

  • 0 = severe deconditioning, composite below 5th percentile
  • 50 = population median for age and sex
  • 100 = top 1% for age and sex
  • Avg American = composed from NHANES component medians (replaces eyeballed 28)

v2 weight derivation

Fit a Cox proportional-hazards model for 10-year all-cause mortality on the eight components in UK Biobank. Take the standardized hazard-ratio coefficients, normalize their absolute values to sum to 1.0, use as weights. This replaces clinical-prior weights with mortality-anchored weights.

Replacement calibration approach

5 · Line render

IN BRIEF The render encodes felt vitality, defined as SF-36 Vitality scale (0 to 100). Base render is a regression of vitality against reserve and lifestyle. Age decay is fit from longitudinal vitality trajectories stratified by reserve, modulating the render mix to produce visible fade across the lifespan. Both fits use UK Biobank cross-sectional and HRS longitudinal data.

Vitality construct

SF-36 Vitality (4 items: feel full of life, have energy, feel worn out, feel tired). Scored 0 to 100, higher = better. Selected over PROMIS Global because of cleaner single-construct definition and longer longitudinal data history.

Base regression

Vitality = α + β_R · Reserve_pct + β_L · Lifestyle_pct + ε Render mix (calibrated) = (β_R · Reserve + β_L · Lifestyle) / (β_R · 100 + β_L · 100)

Replaces current eyeballed 0.5/0.5 weights. Empirical coefficients may be unequal: likely β_L > β_R because lifestyle directly samples felt-state instruments while reserve samples capacity.

Age decay regression

Vitality(age, Reserve) = floor(R) + (1 − floor(R)) / (1 + exp(k(R) · (age − center(R)))) floor(R) = γ₀ + γ₁ · Reserve_pct k(R) = γ₂ + γ₃ · Reserve_pct center(R) = γ₄ + γ₅ · Reserve_pct

Fit by stratifying age trajectories of vitality by reserve quartile in HRS (longitudinal vitality data ages 50 to 95+). Recover γ₀…γ₅ from logistic fits per stratum, then linear fit against continuous reserve.

8 · Open questions and decisions still required

  1. Single cohort vs harmonized. UK Biobank has the depth but is a healthy-volunteer-biased UK adult cohort. Adding HRS and ELSA improves generalization but adds harmonization work.
  2. Stratification beyond sex. v1 is sex-stratified, race-blind. Race/ethnicity stratification is a near-term decision with both validity and ethical implications.
  3. Multi-domain risk vs CVD-only. v1 is CVD via PREVENT. Cancer (LCBC, BCSC), dementia (CAIDE, LIBRA), and fall/fracture (FRAX) all have validated 10-year models. Combining them into one all-event probability vs carrying separately is a real product decision.
  4. DEXA reliance. Reserve leans on DEXA-derived ALMI and bone density; DEXA isn't routine. Substituting BIA and screening-questionnaire bone risk loses precision.
  5. Vitality construct. SF-36 Vitality is older and well-validated; PROMIS Global is newer and modular. v1 picks SF-36 for longitudinal data depth.
  6. Update cadence. Once fitted, weights and reference distributions decay. Annual re-fit against latest NHANES is the floor; quarterly is overkill.
  7. Personalization vs universal. This spec defines the universal teaching tool. The personalized clinical instrument requires different surface decisions (today marker, KPIs, action library) but the model logic carries over.

The current formula should not be described as validated. It is a temporary sketch that needs empirical template replacement.

Logic review

The overall product logic works: Risk, Reserve, Vitality, and Functional Capacity are distinct constructs. Risk represents event probability. Reserve represents biological and functional headroom. Vitality represents lived state. Functional Capacity represents what the person can still do over time.

The current curve logic does not work as a whole: one universal sigmoid cannot represent all profiles. High-reserve trajectories can plausibly look sigmoidal: long high-function plateau, late compression, terminal decline. Low-reserve high-risk trajectories should not start at the same ceiling and then fall like a slide. They likely start lower, decline earlier, decline more steadily, and are heavily affected by mortality selection.

The failed assumption: Risk and Reserve do not merely change the parameters of one curve. They change the shape family. Treating every profile as the same curve with different floor, midpoint, and slope creates visually plausible corners but implausible paths between them.

The current corner-case exercise is useful only as a falsification pass: it can catch obviously impossible outputs. It does not validate the curve. Passing a small set of hand-selected cases is especially weak when the formula has many tunable parameters. The prior 14 of 15 pass claim should be treated as an overfit warning, not evidence of validity.

What should replace the current formula

The next real step is empirical template construction, not more coefficient tuning. Build a small set of reference trajectories from cohort-derived shapes, then interpolate between those shapes.

First-step acceptance criteria

  1. Every extreme slider combination must be inspected as a full curve, not just at selected ages.
  2. Each curve must have a real-world narrative that matches cohort evidence.
  3. If a curve looks clinically wrong, the visual is wrong even if a few numeric checkpoints pass.
  4. The spec must distinguish falsification checks from validation.
  5. The public prototype should not use phrases like calibrated, validated, or model output until a cohort-fit exists.

What stays valid

The durable architecture is the three-axis split: Risk shapes event burden, Reserve shapes the capacity envelope, and Lifestyle contributes to vitality rendering. The banded y-axis also holds up because it maps the abstract number to real-world independence states. What does not hold up is the current single-formula curve.
