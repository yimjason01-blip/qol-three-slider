Functional Capacity Teaching Tool · Spec

A teaching tool that maps physical capability across the lifespan, with three pedagogically independent levers shaping the curve. Built as a chain of decision logs documenting reasoning, tradeoffs, and what is explicitly out of scope.

Purpose

IN BRIEF This spec defines a teaching tool that maps physical capability across the lifespan against age, with three pedagogically independent levers (Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, Underlying Risk) shaping the curve. The y-axis is VO₂max. The line's color encodes felt vitality. The spec is a chain of decision logs documenting why each construct was chosen, what it explicitly does not represent, and what is still open. It is a working spec for an implementable v1, not a validated clinical instrument.

The architecture is built bottom-up through seven decision logs in sequence: Scope and audience → Y-axis → Lever architecture → Vitality color render → Implementation specifications → Modeling decisions → Curve construction. Each one names what it supersedes from prior framings and what propagates into the next. A reader can follow the breadcrumbs from "who is this for" to "exactly how the curve is built" without losing the chain of reasoning.

Scope and audience

IN BRIEF This is a teaching tool for two audiences: patients exploring on their own, and providers using the artifact in clinic as a conversation surface with a patient. It is not a personalized clinical prediction tool. The goal is for both audiences to internalize patterns — how Conditioning shapes the curve, why Lifestyle Optimization shows up mostly as vitality color, what events do to the trajectory — not to predict any individual person's path. Precision target: directionally honest, not patient-fidelity. This shapes every downstream decision.

Two audiences, one artifact

Patient self-explore. Patient moves sliders, watches the curve change, internalizes which lever does what. Walks away with an intuition for "Conditioning is the dominant lever; Lifestyle Optimization shapes how the years feel; Underlying Risk introduces the events." No calibrated number is delivered to the patient. The teaching is the takeaway.

Provider conversation tool. Provider sits with a patient, sets sliders based on a real assessment of that patient's state, uses the curve as a visual aid in care discussion. The provider is not asking the tool "what will happen to this patient." The provider is asking "how do I show this patient what their levers do." Same teaching frame, different room.

What this is not

  1. Not personalized prediction. The tool does not take real lab values, real VO₂max measurements, real risk scores, or real PROs and produce that patient's trajectory. Personalized prediction is a different product with different precision requirements, validation gates, and regulatory exposure.
  2. Not a calibrated clinical instrument. Numbers shown are illustrative, anchored to literature where possible. The curve is directionally honest, not patient-fidelity.
  3. Not a substitute for the patient's actual care plan. A care plan acts on real measurements and real interventions. This tool teaches the structure that makes a care plan readable.

What "directionally honest" means as a precision target

The curve must satisfy three things:

  1. Slider movements produce the right qualitative response. Moving Conditioning from Average to Elite raises the curve and slows decline. Moving Lifestyle Optimization from Maintained to Highly Optimized brightens the line color and slightly buffers slope. Moving Underlying Risk introduces or removes events. The viewer can see what each lever does.
  2. Magnitudes are within published literature ranges. Elite vs sedentary VO₂max gap, MI acute drop, recovery trajectories — all within the bounds of what cohort studies and clinical literature actually show.
  3. The curve does not over-claim. No event happens with certainty. No specific number gets delivered as if it were measured. The "this is one representative path" caption travels with the chart.

What the curve does not need to satisfy: patient-level predictive accuracy, cohort-validated point estimates, statistical confidence intervals on the trajectory. Those are the tests for a clinical prediction tool, which this is not.

Why this matters for downstream decisions

Audience locks precision. Precision locks construction. If the audience were "personalized clinical prediction," the curve would need cohort fitting, validation against held-out data, and confidence rendering. Because the audience is "internalize patterns," the curve construction can rely on empirical reference trajectories with interpolation between them. The tool is allowed to be a teaching artifact, not a calibrated instrument. The Curve Construction decision log makes this concrete.

Defined Terms

This document uses the following terms with specific meanings. They are referenced throughout the body without re-definition. Full reasoning for each is in the decision log sections that follow.

Functional capacity (y-axis)
VO₂max in mL/kg/min, percentile-normed for age and sex against the FRIEND registry. Operationalizes physical capability across the lifespan. Does not represent cognitive capacity. See Y-axis: choice and rationale.
Conditioning
Lever 1. The user's current physical conditioning state, ranged Elite → Well-conditioned → Average → Deconditioned. Implies sustained behavior. Sets curve altitude and decline slope. Dominant lever.
Lifestyle Optimization
Lever 2. Non-training inputs that support vitality: sleep, nutrition, stress and recovery, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence. Two mechanisms: small slope effect on the capacity curve, large effect on the vitality color render.
Underlying Risk
Lever 3. Event-propensity bucket: age, sex, family history, prior disease, imaging findings, inherited variants, environmental exposures, treatment history. Single aggregate slider. Combines with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization into a single effective risk score (R_eff) that drives event onset. The lever's name reflects what it literally represents — the structural and inherited substrate that Conditioning and Lifestyle act on — rather than asking the user to perform a meta-calculation (e.g., subtract fitness's protective effect upstream). The earlier name "Inherited Risk" was rejected as too narrow: the bucket includes acquired-structural factors (prior disease, imaging findings, treatment history) that are not literally inherited. See Modeling Decision 1.
Vitality
Felt experience of being alive at a given physical capacity. Continuous 0 to 100 index. v1 anchored to SF-36 Vitality. Encoded as line color along the curve. Distinct from Functional capacity (curve position).
Render mix
0 to 1 quantity driving line color, brightness, saturation, and weight. v1 form: 0.65·Lifestyle Optimization + 0.35·Conditioning − age_decay − event_acute_drop. Weights are clinical-prior placeholders, replaced in v2 by regression fit.
Cross-talk
Physiological coupling between levers (Conditioning lowers Risk; Lifestyle inputs feed Conditioning over time; events drop Conditioning). The tool declares levers pedagogically orthogonal and discloses cross-talk in a panel rather than modeling it.
Avg American reference
Dashed reference curve representing the median U.S. adult trajectory. Drawn from FRIEND 50th percentile by age and sex with NHANES population health overlays.

Y-axis: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The curve's y-axis is VO₂max in mL/kg/min, percentile-normed for age and sex against the FRIEND registry. This was selected after explicit comparison of nine candidate scales. It is the only single coherent measurement that has resolution at both the upper end of the curve (differentiating elite-fit from recreationally-fit from sedentary healthy adults aged 30 to 70) and the lower end (frailty, ADL dependence, late-life decline), while also having published lifespan trajectory data and physician recognition. The cost of this choice is that cognitive decline is not represented on this axis. That cost is accepted and disclosed.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis is the most propagating decision in the tool. Every band, every lever calibration, every empirical trajectory template, and every claim the tool makes about felt experience depends on what the y-axis measures. Choosing it casually, or as a composite assembled from convenience, weakens every downstream claim. The prior version of this spec defined the y-axis as a 4-domain equal-weighted composite of PROMIS PF, cognition, social participation, and independence. That composite is the failure mode this section corrects.

Why the prior 4-domain composite fails

  1. Frankenstein construction. The four instruments measure incommensurable things on incommensurable scales. Equal weighting is arbitrary and not derived from any underlying model of capacity.
  2. Severe ceiling effect at the top. PROMIS PF v1.2/v2.0 ceilings around T-score 60 to 65 even with the CAT (Hung 2014, Schalet 2016). Lawton IADL and Katz ADL ceiling at maximum independence. Healthy adults aged 30 to 60 all pile near the top of the composite, erasing exactly the granularity the user cares about.
  3. No published lifespan trajectory. No cohort has measured this specific composite from young-adult peak through old-age decline. Any trajectory built on it would be an interpolation across instruments, not an empirical curve.
  4. Not physician-legible as a single number. A clinician cannot translate "your composite is 67" into a clinical assessment, because there is no clinic where this composite is measured.

Constraints the y-axis must satisfy

  1. Single coherent measurement, not a composite of unrelated instruments.
  2. Top-end resolution. Must distinguish elite-fit from recreationally-fit from sedentary healthy adults aged 30 to 70.
  3. Bottom-end resolution. Must map cleanly to frailty, ADL dependence, and care-need thresholds.
  4. Lifespan trajectory data. Published longitudinal data from age 20 to 90+, ideally from a single cohort.
  5. Physician-legible. A clinician should recognize the number and the bands without explanation.
  6. Honest about what it does not measure. The scale should not pretend to capture dimensions it cannot.

The irreducible tradeoff in the field

The literature has no instrument that satisfies all six constraints. Top-end granularity and bottom-end clinical meaning are inversely correlated across every validated scale. Anything calibrated to detect ADL loss (Intrinsic Capacity, SPPB, 6MWD, PROMIS PF, Healthy Aging Index, Frailty Index) ceilings before age 50 in healthy adults. Anything that resolves elite fitness (VO₂max, power output, masters-athlete percentiles) maps imperfectly onto disability cutoffs. This gap is the field's gap, not this tool's. The choice is which side of the tradeoff to honor.

Why VO₂max wins

  1. Top-end resolution. Elite endurance 70 to 85 mL/kg/min, recreationally fit 40 to 50, sedentary 25 to 35, pre-frail 20, ADL-threshold 15, bedbound <10. Continuous, no ceiling effect across the range that matters.
  2. Bottom-end resolution. VO₂max <17.5 (men) and <15.5 (women) maps to functional dependence (Paterson and Warburton 2010). The same number that distinguishes recreational riders from competitive ones distinguishes "lives alone safely" from "needs daily help."
  3. Lifespan trajectory data. Fleg et al. (BLSA, Circulation 2005) published longitudinal VO₂max trajectories from age 20 to 90+ with accelerating decline (~3 to 6%/decade in 20s, >20%/decade after 70). Kaminsky et al. (Mayo Clin Proc 2015, 2022) published the FRIEND registry percentile norms.
  4. Physician recognition. AHA 2016 statement (Ross et al., Circulation) designated cardiorespiratory fitness as a clinical vital sign. Mandsager 2018 (JAMA Netw Open) showed CRF predicts all-cause mortality more strongly than smoking, hypertension, or diabetes. Used in cardio rehab, surgical risk stratification, and longevity medicine practice.
  5. Single coherent measurement. Not a composite. One physiological quantity, measurable directly via CPET, estimable via submaximal protocols.
  6. Lever responsiveness is well-characterized. Training raises VO₂max ~15 to 25%. Sedentary lifestyle accelerates decline ~2x. Smoking, untreated disease, and chronic inflammation drop it. The levers in this tool can be calibrated against published intervention literature.

What this choice explicitly does not measure

Cognitive decline is not represented on this axis. A person with preserved cardiorespiratory fitness and progressive dementia will appear "fine" on this curve. Sensory loss (vision, hearing) is similarly not captured. Specific neuromuscular power (which declines faster than VO₂max in late life, Reid and Fielding 2012) is approximated but not directly measured.

The tool will disclose this on the visible surface. The framing: "This shows the cardiorespiratory and physical capability curve. Cognitive trajectory is a separate axis the tool does not render." A composite that pretended to capture cognition would have been the worse choice, because it would have been the Frankenstein this section rejects.

Bands

Bands are capability-verb statements anchored to VO₂max ranges. Numbers appear as small left-axis ticks. Bands are the semantic ground in the chart background. The boundaries are continuous gradients, not hard cuts, because VO₂max is continuous.

Visual treatment of the axis

  1. VO₂max linear on the y-axis. The cliff bands at the bottom will look narrow because the cliff is short and steep. That visual narrowness is the truth.
  2. Single axis, not two-sided. Numbers as ticks, bands as zones. Two-sided axes split attention.
  3. Bands as gradient zones in the background, not stacked colored bars with sharp lines. The viewer should feel the transition, not see a step.
  4. Band names render as floating text inside each zone. Capability-verb language, not sport-codes.

Open questions deferred to implementation

  1. Whether to also display sex-specific bands or absolute VO₂max only. Absolute is simpler and clinically standard; percentile-normed is more honest about relative position. Likely answer: absolute on the axis, percentile in the readout.
  2. Whether the cognitive-decline disclaimer lives as a permanent caption, a footnote, or a one-time onboarding card. Argues for caption, since the disclosure must travel with the chart.
  3. How to handle the very-old tail (90+) where measurement protocols become unreliable. Likely answer: render dashed beyond age 90 to signal increasing uncertainty.
  4. Whether avg American reference uses FRIEND 50th percentile by age, NHANES estimated VO₂max, or BLSA cohort means. Likely answer: FRIEND, because it is the most current and largest U.S. reference dataset.

Lever architecture: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The three levers that shape the curve are Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, and Underlying Risk. Conditioning is a state slider (elite to deconditioned) whose position implies sustained behavior; it sets curve altitude and decline slope. Lifestyle Optimization covers non-training inputs that support vitality (sleep, nutrition, stress and recovery, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence) and has two mechanisms: a small effect on decline slope, and a large effect on the vitality color render of the line. Underlying Risk is the event-propensity bucket (genetics, prior disease, environmental exposure) that combines with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization into a single effective risk score driving event onset. The architecture replaces the prior Risk / Reserve / Lifestyle naming, which had unresolved overlap with the new VO₂max y-axis.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis decision propagates directly into how levers are defined. Once the y-axis is VO₂max, the prior Reserve lever (an 8-component capacity composite that included cardiorespiratory fitness) collapses into the y-axis itself: the lever and the chart axis would be measuring the same construct. The prior Risk definition (residualized against capacity) becomes circular: residualizing against the chart itself. The prior Lifestyle definition included training behavior, which is now redundant with Conditioning. The lever set must be redrawn so each slider has a distinct mechanism and no double-counting.

Why the prior Risk / Reserve / Lifestyle framing fails under the new y-axis

  1. Reserve overlapped the y-axis. The cardiorespiratory component of Reserve is VO₂max, which is now the y-axis. The lever and the dependent variable measured the same thing.
  2. Risk residualization became circular. "Risk not explained by capacity" requires capacity to be a separate construct. When capacity is the chart, the residualization has nothing clean to residualize against.
  3. Lifestyle conflated physical and non-physical inputs. Sleep and training behavior were in the same bucket despite operating through different mechanisms with different evidence bases.

The three levers, redrawn

Conditioning · state slider

Range: Elite → Well-conditioned → Average → Deconditioned.

What it represents: The user's current physical conditioning state, with the implicit assumption that the slider position reflects sustained behavior. Setting the slider to Elite means the person lives in a way that produces and maintains elite conditioning. Setting it to Deconditioned means sustained low activity.

Mechanism on the curve: Sets baseline VO₂max anchor and decline slope. This is the dominant lever. At age 50, Elite-conditioned adults sit roughly 15 to 20 mL/kg/min above Average peers and 25 to 30 mL/kg/min above Deconditioned peers. Trained adults decline roughly 30 to 50% slower per decade through midlife than untrained peers, with the gap narrowing in late life as terminal compression dominates (Hawkins and Wiswell 2003, Pollock 1987, BLSA top quintile vs cohort means).

Why a state, not a behavior: Training is the input; conditioning is the result. Only the result drives the curve. A separate training slider would double-count.

Why this is not the same as the y-axis: The slider is set once, by the user, as a self-assessment. The y-axis is the rendered VO₂max trajectory at every age. The slider says "this is who I am"; the curve says "this is how that trajectory unfolds."

Lifestyle Optimization · non-training inputs that support vitality

Range: Highly Optimized → Maintained → Neglected.

What it represents: Non-training behavioral and medical inputs that support vitality. Sleep behavior, nutrition, stress and recovery load, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence. Does not include training behavior — that lives in Conditioning.

Why "Optimization" rather than "Lifestyle": The lever covers both passive behaviors (sleep, social) and active medical layers (hormone panels, metabolic optimization, supplementation). "Lifestyle" alone reads as passive description. "Optimization" reads as deliberate stance, which is what the slider position represents — how much effort someone puts into vitality-supporting inputs across both behavioral and medical domains.

Why hormone management lives here: Hormone optimization (testosterone, estrogen, thyroid, metabolic panels) belongs in the same bucket as other vitality-supporting inputs because it operates through the same mechanism: supporting felt energy, mood, recovery, drive, and connection. It is not the only medical optimization input, but it is named in the lever definition because it is a meaningful and increasingly common practice in the longevity-medicine space.

Mechanism on the curve, dual:

  1. Small effect on slope. Sleep deprivation, chronic stress, and social isolation accelerate VO₂max decline; the inverse buffers it. Effect size is real but modest and weakly calibrated against VO₂max trajectory specifically. ±5 to 10% slope modulation is the defensible range.
  2. Large effect on vitality color render. The same inputs have well-documented effects on felt vitality (SF-36 Vitality, energy, mood, drive). This is the lever's primary mechanism. See the Vitality decision log section for full treatment.

Why this lever still matters even though its slope effect is small: Vitality is a first-class outcome the tool exists to teach. A patient at VO₂max 35 can feel vital or depleted based on this lever, and that difference matters more in lived experience than a 5% slope change. Lifestyle Optimization is also the lever a Deconditioned person can move first, before they can credibly move Conditioning.

Underlying Risk · event-propensity bucket

Range: Low → Average → High.

What it represents: Event-propensity factors not directly captured by Conditioning or Lifestyle Optimization in the slider's mechanical effect on the curve. Includes age, sex, family history, prior disease, imaging findings (CAC, plaque, BMD, liver fat), inherited variants, environmental exposures, and structural disease burden.

Mechanism on the curve: Combined with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization into a single effective risk score (R_eff), which drives event probability and modal age. Both effects compound: higher Underlying Risk makes events more likely and earlier. Conditioning is a meaningful protective input — fitness lowers R_eff, which both lowers each event's lifetime probability and pushes the modal age later. See Modeling Decisions for the formula and joint-outcome model.

Why "Underlying Risk" rather than a residualized framing: a prior framing required users to mentally subtract fitness's protective effect before setting the slider — a meta-calculation that fails the teaching tool's "what the slider says is what the lever does" usability bar. The current name reflects what the lever literally represents: the structural and inherited substrate that the other two levers act on. An earlier draft named the lever "Inherited Risk," but the bucket genuinely contains acquired-structural factors — prior disease, imaging findings, treatment history — that are not literally inherited; the rename closes that gap. Conditioning's protective effect is now visible in the slider behavior (move Conditioning up, R_eff drops, events shift later and become less likely) rather than required to be performed by the user upstream.

Lever weight: not equal

Conditioning is the dominant lever. It sets both baseline and slope, and is the heaviest input to the effective risk score driving event onset (Mandsager 2018). Lifestyle Optimization modulates slope at the margins and contributes a small amount to event onset. Underlying Risk is the dominant input to event onset at high-risk profiles. The viewer will see Conditioning move the curve more than the other two. This is correct and reflects the literature.

Independence: pedagogical, not physiological

In nature the levers are correlated. The R_eff combination in Modeling Decision 1 represents the most evidenced cross-effect (Conditioning lowers cardiovascular event risk). Other cross-talk — Lifestyle inputs feeding Conditioning over years; events dropping Conditioning in reality — remains in the disclosure panel rather than being modeled. The tool aims for the lightest model that honors the strongest links and is honest about the rest.

What this architecture gives the viewer

Three sliders, three visually distinct effects on the curve. Conditioning shifts altitude, tilts the slope, and protects against events (lowers R_eff). Lifestyle Optimization bends the slope subtly and drives the line color (vitality). Underlying Risk dominates R_eff at high-risk profiles, raising lifetime probability and pulling modal age earlier for each forking event. A patient sees what each lever does on its own; a provider sees how to weight a care plan across the three. This is the teaching payload the tool exists to deliver.

Open questions deferred to modeling decisions

All open questions from the prior version of this section have been resolved in the Modeling Decisions log:

  • Stochastic vs deterministic events: resolved as deterministic-probabilistic — same sliders always produce the same four-branch curve, but the trajectory shows multiple probability-weighted futures rather than a single representative path. See Modeling Decision 2.
  • Event scope: resolved as cancer treatment + CV event for v1 forks; neurodegenerative as dashed-segment visual; other domains deferred to v2. See Modeling Decision 3.
  • Recovery dynamics: resolved as three-parameter (acute drop, time constant, asymptotic recovery fraction) per event class. See Modeling Decision 4.
  • Conditioning ↔ Lifestyle independence: declared orthogonal by construction; cross-talk disclosed in panel. See Modeling Decision 5.
  • Conditioning → Risk reduction: resolved as modeled (the R_eff combination formula). See Modeling Decision 1.

Vitality color render: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The chart renders two layers, not one. The line's position is the VO₂max trajectory (capacity over time). The line's color is felt vitality at each point of the trajectory. Vitality is operationalized as a continuous index drawing from validated patient-reported outcome instruments covering energy, mood, drive, connection, and pain. Conditioning contributes meaningfully to vitality because cardiovascular fitness genuinely supports felt energy, but Lifestyle Optimization is the dominant input. Vitality fades with age regardless of conditioning, because lived experience does. The render exists because capacity and vitality are genuinely different outcomes and a single curve cannot show both.

Why this decision matters

Capacity (what your body can do) and vitality (how living that life feels) are not the same. A 60-year-old at VO₂max 35 can feel alive or depleted depending on sleep, mood, hormones, pain, and connection. A 30-year-old at VO₂max 50 can be high-capacity and exhausted. The teaching tool exists in part to communicate that distinction. Without a vitality render, the tool would teach only physical capability and would miss the lived-experience layer that physicians and patients actually care about.

The vitality render is also what gives Lifestyle Optimization its primary mechanism. The slope effect of Lifestyle Optimization on VO₂max trajectory is real but modest and weakly calibrated. The color effect is large and well-supported. Without the vitality render, Lifestyle Optimization would be a weak lever. With it, the lever has a primary visual signature that reflects its real-world importance.

What vitality is, and what it is not

Vitality is: the felt experience of being alive at a given physical capacity. Energy, mood, drive, connection, sense of purpose, freedom from pain and fatigue, libido, presence. Downstream of physiology and behavior, but a first-class outcome in its own right.

Vitality is not: physical capability, cognitive function, or risk of disease. It is not measured by VO₂max, by ADL count, by frailty index, or by mortality probability. A person can have high vitality at any physical capacity, and low vitality at any physical capacity. The independence is not perfect, but it is real.

Operationalization

Vitality is a continuous 0 to 100 index drawing from validated patient-reported outcome dimensions. v1 anchors the construct to SF-36 Vitality (4 items: feel full of life, have energy, feel worn out, feel tired) for its single-construct definition and longitudinal data history. v2 broadens to a multi-instrument composite covering vitality dimensions not captured by SF-36 alone: PROMIS Emotional Distress (mood), PROMIS Social Health (connection), brief sexual function (drive), pain interference, and purpose-in-life. The v2 composite is acknowledged as composite construction, but is justified here because vitality is genuinely multidimensional in lived experience — collapsing to one instrument would underrepresent it.

Why a composite is acceptable here when a composite was rejected for the y-axis: The y-axis decision required a single coherent measurement because the curve is the dependent variable and any composite would have inherited the Frankenstein problem (incommensurable scales, arbitrary weights, no published trajectory data). Vitality as render is a secondary visual layer informed by a composite is acceptable because the render does not need a published lifespan trajectory. It needs to encode the directional signal of felt experience at each point of the curve. Different evidence requirement, different acceptable construction.

Inputs to vitality

Vitality at age A is a function of three inputs:

  1. Lifestyle Optimization — dominant input. Sleep, nutrition, stress and recovery, social connection, substance exposure, hormone and metabolic optimization, medical adherence all directly drive felt vitality. This is where the lever has its primary teaching signal.
  2. Conditioning — meaningful contribution. Cardiovascular fitness genuinely supports felt energy and recovery. A Highly Optimized but Deconditioned person will not feel as vital as a Highly Optimized and Well-conditioned person, because the body itself contributes to felt aliveness. The render mix should reflect this.
  3. Age — vitality fades with age regardless of the other two inputs, because lived experience does. Even at peak optimization and elite conditioning, vitality at age 85 is lower than at age 35. The age decay is gentle for high-optimization, high-conditioning profiles and steeper for low ones.

Underlying Risk does not directly drive vitality between events. Events themselves cause acute vitality drops via pain, recovery burden, and psychological impact; per Decision 2, each event also leaves a permanent vitality residual matching its capacity residual. The vitality render carries this per branch — branches that experienced events show muted color late in life, branches that didn't show clean color.

Render mix, v1 form

The render mix is a 0 to 1 quantity that drives line color, brightness, saturation, and weight. It is computed per branch in the joint-outcome model — each branch carries only the events that fired in that branch, so the spared branch and the both-events branch can show different colors at the same age. v1 form:

render_mix(age, branch) = 0.65 · Lifestyle_Optimization 
                        + 0.35 · Conditioning_contribution
                        − age_decay(age, Lifestyle, Conditioning)
                        − vitality_event_burden(age, branch_events)

The 0.65 / 0.35 weight is a clinical-prior placeholder, not a calibrated parameter. The direction of the asymmetry (Lifestyle Optimization > Conditioning) is supported by the literature: PROs that anchor felt vitality respond more strongly to sleep, mood, and connection inputs than to fitness inputs. v2 calibration replaces the 0.65 / 0.35 weights with regression coefficients fit against the vitality composite outcome in UK Biobank cross-sectional and HRS longitudinal data.

The vitality_event_burden term mirrors the capacity model from Modeling Decision 4: each event applies an acute vitality drop that recovers exponentially toward a permanent residual penalty. The acute vitality drop is scaled to ~60% of the event's capacity drop fraction (so a 25% capacity drop produces a visible but not catastrophic vitality dent on the 0–1 scale); the recovery time constant and recovery fraction match the capacity model exactly. Cancer treatment leaves a vitality residual proportional to its capacity residual (per Jones 2012, cancer survivors carry chronic fatigue and PRO decrements that match the VO₂max drop direction). CV event leaves a smaller residual (cardiac rehab cohorts recover most felt-vitality outcomes within 12 months, but post-MI depression and chronic anxiety persist). The residual fractions are the same as the capacity residuals: 80% of acute vitality drop persists for cancer, 25% persists for CV.

Because vitality is computed per branch, the spared branch shows clean late-life color while the both-events branch shows the cumulative event color burden — the two viewers see different stories at the same age, which is the whole point of rendering branches as separate trajectories. This is what Modeling Decision 2 promises ("the 'both' branch's color late in life reflects the cumulative event burden"); the per-branch vitality formula is what delivers it.

Visual treatment

  1. Color ramp: red (depleted) through neutral through green (vital). Red-green ramp tested for color-blind safety; alternative is dark-purple to bright-amber if red-green is rejected.
  2. Brightness and saturation co-vary with the render mix. High vitality is bright and saturated; low vitality is muted and desaturated.
  3. Line weight scales subtly with vitality. High vitality reads as a confident, full line; low vitality reads as a thinner, less-present line.
  4. No fill underneath the curve. The line itself carries the render. Filled area under the curve would compete visually with the band zones in the chart background.
  5. Per-segment rendering. The curve is sampled at fine intervals (every 0.5 years) and each segment is colored by the vitality at that age. The viewer sees a gradient along the curve, not a monochrome line.

Why Conditioning contributes to vitality, not just Lifestyle Optimization

The prior version of this model attempted to make Lifestyle the sole render input and treat Conditioning as render-neutral. That construction is not honest. Cardiovascular fitness contributes to felt energy in a way the longevity-medicine literature consistently documents. A Highly Optimized but Deconditioned person feels less vital than a Highly Optimized and Well-conditioned person, holding everything else constant. The render mix therefore must include Conditioning. The 0.65 / 0.35 split is the v1 estimate; the precise weight is a v2 calibration target.

The cross-talk this creates with the lever architecture

Conditioning now has two effects: capacity (its primary role) and vitality (its secondary role). This means moving the Conditioning slider visibly changes both the line position and the line color. That is correct and reflects reality. It does mean Conditioning is even more dominant in the model than the lever architecture section already acknowledged. The "three sliders, three independent effects" frame becomes "three sliders, four to five distinct visible effects." The teaching frame still holds — the viewer sees what each lever does — but the model is honest that Conditioning's reach is larger than a single slider's worth of effect.

Open questions deferred to implementation

  1. Whether v1 ships with SF-36 Vitality as the sole anchor, or with the broader v2 composite from the start. Argues for SF-36 in v1, composite as a v2 target documented in the spec.
  2. Whether age decay parameters differ by sex. Likely yes, because vitality trajectories differ; deferred to calibration.
  3. Whether the color ramp has a fixed scale (e.g., 0 to 100 vitality maps to red-to-green absolutely) or a relative scale (the user's own range across slider settings). Likely fixed, because relative would let any setting look bright.

Implementation specifications

IN BRIEF Implementation-level specifications that the prior decision logs imply but do not state precisely. Slider granularity, vitality baseline at the youngest rendered age, and the disambiguation between Lifestyle Optimization (a lever the user moves) and metabolic syndrome (a state inside Underlying Risk). These are not new decisions; they are precise statements of what the prior decision logs commit to so that implementation has no judgment calls left.

Slider granularity

All three sliders are continuous on a 0 to 100 internal scale. The named labels (Elite, Well-conditioned, Average, Deconditioned for Conditioning; Highly Optimized, Maintained, Neglected for Lifestyle Optimization; Low, Average, High for Underlying Risk) are visual markers along the slider track, not stepped positions. The slider snaps visually to the nearest named marker only on release for accessibility, but the underlying value is continuous.

  • Conditioning: 0 = Deconditioned (15th percentile FRIEND), 33 = Average (50th percentile), 66 = Well-conditioned (~80th percentile), 100 = Elite (95th percentile). Maps to the v1 reference trajectory templates from the Curve Construction section.
  • Lifestyle Optimization: 0 = Neglected, 50 = Maintained, 100 = Highly Optimized. Slope multiplier interpolates linearly: 0 → 1.10× decline rate, 50 → 1.00×, 100 → 0.90× (the ±10% bound).
  • Underlying Risk: 0 to 100 continuous. The slider value combines with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization into the effective risk score (R_eff) per Modeling Decision 1; R_eff drives both lifetime probability and modal age for each forking event per Modeling Decision 2.

Vitality baseline at the youngest rendered age

The chart renders from age 30 to age 100. At age 30, vitality at peak inputs (Conditioning = Elite, Lifestyle Optimization = Highly Optimized) is 95 on the 0 to 100 scale, not 100. The 5-point gap below the ceiling represents the irreducible variability in felt experience even at peak inputs (energy fluctuates day to day, life stress exists, etc.). At age 30 with worst inputs (Deconditioned, Neglected), vitality is ~45. The age-decay term in the render mix attenuates from this baseline downward.

Why not 100 at peak inputs: a render that hits the ceiling at peak inputs implies the tool is claiming "this is the best a 30-year-old can feel." It cannot defensibly make that claim. A 5-point ceiling gap is honest and visually invisible.

Lifestyle Optimization (lever) vs metabolic syndrome (state)

Two related but distinct constructs that could be confused without explicit disambiguation:

  • Lifestyle Optimization is a lever the user moves. It includes "hormone and metabolic optimization" as a behavior — sleep, nutrition, stress management, lab-driven hormone tuning, metabolic health support. It is what the person does.
  • Metabolic syndrome is a state inside Underlying Risk. It is the clinical syndrome (elevated waist circumference, BP, fasting glucose, triglycerides, low HDL) that elevates event probability across cardiovascular, kidney, and cancer domains. It is what is true of the person's body.

The relationship between them: Lifestyle Optimization (the lever) is one of the determinants of whether metabolic syndrome (the state) develops. Sustained Highly Optimized lifestyle reduces metabolic syndrome probability; sustained Neglected lifestyle increases it. Genetics, age, and prior disease also contribute. Once metabolic syndrome is present, it elevates event frequency through Underlying Risk's contribution to R_eff. The user models a metabolic-syndrome-present body by moving Underlying Risk up, and models the upstream behavior that drives or prevents that state by moving Lifestyle Optimization. This split matches reality: behavior produces state over years, and the tool is rendering one moment in time.

What the cross-talk panel discloses: "Lifestyle Optimization affects whether metabolic syndrome and other risk states develop over time. The tool models the lever and the state separately. Move Lifestyle Optimization to set behavior; move Underlying Risk to set the state." This is one of the entries in the cross-talk panel from Modeling Decision 1.

Cross-talk panel placement

The panel is a permanently visible expandable element below the chart, not a one-time onboarding card. The default state shows a single line: "Three things this tool simplifies. Open." Clicking expands the second-order links the model does not directly represent (events drop conditioning in reality; lifestyle inputs feed conditioning over years; Underlying Risk is a single dial but real risk profiles are domain-specific). This placement ensures the disclosure travels with the chart without dominating the visual surface.

Modeling decisions: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF Each lever has a primary mechanism on the curve, but Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, and Underlying Risk also combine into a single effective risk score (R_eff) that drives event onset — fitness's protective effect is too well-evidenced to hide in a disclosure panel. Events are probabilistic forks, not deterministic step-downs: at each event's modal age the trajectory splits into a "no-event" branch and a "with-event" branch. v1 ships two forking events (cancer treatment, CV event), which generate four joint outcomes (spared both, cancer only, CV only, both) that render as four full-weight lines from their fork point to age 100. R_eff drives both lifetime probability AND modal age for each event — high underlying risk makes events more likely and earlier, both effects compound. Each event = acute drop with asymptotic recovery to a permanent residual; residuals carry on both capacity (curve position) and vitality (line color). v1 also renders neurodegenerative onset as a dashed segment past R_eff-dependent age. Other Meridian domains (CKD, HF, hip, stroke, dialysis, metabolic) are deferred to v2 with the per-domain Underlying Risk decomposition.

Why this decision matters

The y-axis and lever architecture sections fixed what the tool measures and which levers shape it. This section fixes how the levers map to the curve mechanically and which events appear. These are the last decisions before code. Each one propagates: the orthogonality choice changes how cross-talk is communicated; the event scope changes the curve's visual signature; the event-firing model changes whether the curve is reproducible across renders. Locking these here means implementation does not invent answers under deadline.

Decision 1 · Levers combine into a single effective risk score

Choice: Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, and Underlying Risk combine into a single continuous score R_eff that drives event onset. Each lever has a primary visible mechanism on the curve plus contributes to R_eff. The earlier "mutual exclusivity by construction" decision is reverted on this specific link, because the most-evidenced relationship in the entire model — VO₂max → cardiovascular event risk — is too central to hide in a disclosure panel.

Formula:

R_eff = 0.55 · (Underlying_Risk / 100)
      + 0.35 · (1 − Conditioning / 100)
      + 0.10 · (1 − Lifestyle_Optimization / 100)

R_eff is bounded 0 to 1. Weights are clinical-prior placeholders, not calibrated. Underlying Risk is weighted heaviest because severe genetic and structural loading (familial hypercholesterolemia, BRCA carriers, prior MI, severe CAC, etc.) cannot be overridden by fitness alone. Conditioning is the second-heaviest input, anchored to the CRF/mortality gradient (Mandsager 2018; Ross 2016 AHA statement). Lifestyle Optimization has a small honest contribution — sleep, stress, and metabolic optimization affect event probability but with smaller measured effect sizes than CRF.

Sanity checks use the joint-outcome math from Decision 2: each R_eff produces a CV probability and a cancer probability, which combine into four joint outcome probabilities summing to 100%.

  • Avg American (33/50/50): R_eff = 0.56. Cancer ~41%, CV ~31%. Joint outcomes: 41% spared, 28% cancer only, 18% CV only, 13% both. Cancer modal age 65, CV modal age 75. Matches population-level event rates.
  • Elite/Optimized/Max-UR (100/100/100): R_eff = 0.55. Cancer ~40%, CV ~30%. Joint outcomes: 42% spared, 28% cancer only, 18% CV only, 12% both. Modal ages essentially identical to the average American. The lesson: maximum protective behaviors bring a max-UR person down to population-average R_eff — not below it. Genetics still matters, but fitness and optimization are the levers that close the gap.
  • Elite/Maintained/Avg (100/50/50): R_eff = 0.33. Cancer ~26%, CV ~12%. Joint outcomes: 65% spared, 23% cancer only, 9% CV only, 3% both. Cancer modal age 70, CV modal age 81. The lesson: getting fit at average underlying risk dramatically shifts probability toward "spared both" and pushes the events that do happen later.
  • Decon/Neglected/Min-UR (0/0/0): R_eff = 0.45. Cancer ~34%, CV ~22%. Joint outcomes: 51% spared, 27% cancer only, 15% CV only, 7% both. Cancer modal age 67, CV modal age 78. The lesson: bad behavior alone, with zero inherited loading, still produces meaningful event risk because behavior itself drives R_eff. The curve is also low (capacity is poor) regardless of which branch the patient lives on.

The four corner cases collectively validate that the R_eff weights produce defensible probability surfaces across the slider grid, with no degenerate corners where the math breaks down or contradicts literature.

What this overrides: the prior "mutual exclusivity by construction" decision. That decision said each lever has exactly one mechanism on the curve and physiological cross-talk goes in a disclosure panel. Reverted only for the Conditioning → event-risk link, because:

  1. The link is first-order, not cross-talk. Mandsager 2018 and the AHA CRF-as-vital-sign statement (Ross 2016) make this one of the strongest predictive relationships in the literature. Putting it in a disclosure panel under-represents the evidence base.
  2. The "Underlying Risk" construct that the prior decision required is non-obvious. A teaching tool whose slider requires the user to perform a meta-calculation before setting it ("subtract fitness's protective effect") fails the usability test. Either the slider is what its name says, or the tool is broken.
  3. Without this link, the tool teaches that fitness does not protect against events. Patients and providers will reject this and rightly so.

What stays in mutual exclusivity: the levers' primary mechanisms remain distinct. Conditioning sets curve altitude and slope. Lifestyle Optimization drives line color (vitality) and a small slope multiplier. Underlying Risk is the dominant input to event onset. The R_eff combination is one specific channel for cross-effect (event onset), not a global muddying of all mechanisms. The cross-talk panel still discloses the second-order links the model does not represent (events drop conditioning in reality; lifestyle inputs feed conditioning over years).

Decision 2 · Joint-outcome divergent timelines

Choice: Two independent forking events (cancer treatment at modal age 65, CV event at modal age 75) generate four joint outcomes. Each outcome is a real future the patient could live, with its own joint probability and its own permanent trajectory. All four render as full-weight lines from their fork point to age 100. No reconvergence. No probability-weighted "expected" curve. The four lines are the model.

The four joint outcomes:

P(spared both)  = (1 − P_cancer) · (1 − P_cv)
P(cancer only)  = P_cancer · (1 − P_cv)
P(CV only)      = (1 − P_cancer) · P_cv
P(both events)  = P_cancer · P_cv

Joint probabilities always sum to 100%. Outcomes whose probability rounds below 0.5% are dropped from render as noise.

Why this replaces the prior dominant/subordinate-with-reconvergence model: The prior version chose a "dominant" branch per fork (whichever had probability > 50%) and reconverged the subordinate branch back into the dominant trunk after a visibility window. That construction had three problems:

  1. It silently discarded probability mass. When cancer was the subordinate branch (P < 50%), the cancer-treated trajectory disappeared from the chart at the reconvergence age. The 20% permanent residual loss specified in Decision 4 existed in the math but vanished from the visual, contradicting the recovery dynamics decision.
  2. It editorialized which future is canonical. Opacity-weighting branches by probability told the viewer "this future is more likely so look here." For a teaching tool meant to make patients sit with both possibilities equally, that frames the chart against its own purpose.
  3. It hid the "both events" outcome entirely. With sequential reconvergence, the second fork only branched off whichever first-fork branch was dominant. A patient experiencing both cancer treatment AND a CV event was never rendered. At average risk this is ~12% probability mass — not a corner case.

Two events fork in v1: cardiovascular (MI/stroke composite) and cancer treatment. These two cover the dominant 80/20 of mortality and capacity disruption, and at four joint outcomes the chart remains readable. Hip fracture, HF onset, and dialysis initiation are deferred to v2, when Underlying Risk fragments into per-domain sliders. Adding more forking events would multiply the outcome count (3 events = 8 outcomes, 4 events = 16) past visual saturation.

Independence assumption: v1 treats cancer and CV as independent. Real cardio-oncology cross-talk (cardiotoxicity raising CV risk in cancer survivors; shared metabolic substrate raising both) is acknowledged as a v2 calibration target. The independence simplification is honest about its limit and matches the teaching frame: the patient learns that both events can happen on the same trajectory, even if the joint probability v1 reports is slightly off from reality.

Probability and modal age both shift with R_eff: Higher underlying risk does two things — raises lifetime probability AND pulls modal age earlier. Both effects compound. v1 lifetime probabilities and modal ages are pulled from published cohort literature stratified by R_eff equivalent. v2 replaces the R_eff → (probability, age) mapping with cohort-fit functions. v1 anchors:

EventR_eff = 0.30 (Low)R_eff = 0.55 (Avg)R_eff = 0.80 (High)Source
CV event probability~10%~30%~55%PREVENT (Khan 2024); Pooled Cohort Equations
CV event modal age827567PREVENT age-stratified incidence; FH literature (MI ~10-20yr earlier in high-risk)
Cancer treatment probability~25%~40%~55%SEER cumulative incidence by age and site; treatment-burden assumption ~70% of incidence requires capacity-affecting treatment
Cancer treatment modal age706558SEER stratified incidence; hereditary syndrome literature (BRCA, Lynch ~10-20yr earlier)

Both probabilities and modal ages interpolate continuously between the three R_eff anchor points. The teaching: at high risk, events are not just more likely — they happen sooner. The two effects together compress healthy years from both ends of the curve.

Branch trajectory math: Each joint outcome's capacity at age A equals the base capacity at A multiplied by the product of event factors for events that fired in that outcome. Each event factor follows the acute-drop-with-asymptotic-recovery curve in Decision 4: drops to 1 − drop at the modal age, recovers exponentially toward 1 − residual with time constant τ. The residual penalty persists for the rest of the trajectory. A branch that took cancer treatment never returns to the spared branch's altitude. A branch that took both events sits below all three other branches permanently.

Vitality per branch: Each outcome carries its own vitality render. The "both" branch's color late in life reflects the cumulative event burden on felt experience, not the hypothetical "no events" trajectory. Different branches can therefore display different colors at the same age — the spared branch reads vital while the both-events branch reads depleted.

Render rules:

  1. All four lines render at full visual weight. No opacity weighting by probability. The labels carry the probability information.
  2. Each line is colored by its own per-segment vitality. Same vitality formula as Decision in the Vitality color render section, applied per-branch.
  3. Right-edge labels: joint probability and outcome name (spared / cancer only / CV only / both), stacked in y-order to avoid crossings, color-matched to each line's terminal vitality.
  4. Fork markers: small dots on the spared trajectory at each event's modal age, with a faint vertical guide line. Event name labels above the chart.
  5. Outcomes with rounded probability < 0.5% are dropped from render. At low Underlying Risk + high Conditioning, the "both" outcome can be near 1-2% — small enough to be visually marginal but kept in the render so the teaching that "both can happen on any profile" stays honest.

What this construction is honest about: the four lines on the chart represent four mutually exclusive futures with their joint probabilities. The chart does not claim any patient will live on any specific line. The teaching: "Your slider settings produce these four possible trajectories with these probabilities. The probability you experience each one shifts as your sliders move."

What this construction admits: visual compression at the low-capacity end of the y-axis. When all four branches sit between 5-15 mL/kg/min in late life (e.g. high-risk sedentary profile), the lines bunch. That is the truth — at that capacity range, event differentials are real but small in absolute mL/kg/min terms. Y-axis is linear by Y-axis decision; the compression is a feature, not a bug.

Stacking semantics: within a multi-event branch (the "both" outcome), event factors compound multiplicatively per Decision 4. A 25% CV drop on top of a 20% permanent cancer residual leaves capacity at 0.80 × 0.75 = 0.60 of the no-event baseline at the time of the CV event, recovering toward 0.80 × 0.9375 = 0.75 after CV recovery (since CV's residual is 6.25%). Multiplicative stacking preserves the teaching that Conditioning still buys altitude even on the worst joint outcome — Elite/HighR's both-events branch lands meaningfully higher than Decon/HighR's both-events branch, because the same fractional drops apply to a higher starting capacity.

Caption on the chart: "Two events fork the trajectory: cancer treatment (~age 65) and CV event (~age 75). Four real futures render: spared both, cancer only, CV only, both. Joint probabilities sum to 100%."

Decision 3 · Event scope by Meridian risk domain

Choice: v1 ships two forking events (cancer treatment, CV event) plus a neurodegenerative dashed-segment visual. The other Meridian domains (CKD, HF onset, hip fracture, stroke as a separate fork, dialysis initiation, metabolic syndrome) are deferred to v2. Decision 2 explains the visual-budget constraint that caps v1 at two forking events. This section explains, for each domain, the modeling stance that v1 takes and what v2 will add.

Cardiovascular · v1 forks (MI/stroke composite)

v1 ships the CV fork as an MI/stroke composite at the modal ages and probabilities anchored in Decision 2. The composite framing collapses MI and stroke into one event because both are first-order capacity disruptions with similar acute-drop magnitude (20–30%) and broadly similar recovery shapes (partial, with permanent residual). v2 splits them into separate forks once the per-domain Underlying Risk decomposition arrives. HF onset is deferred — its mechanism (progressive decline interleaved with decompensations) does not fit the single-acute-drop event model and needs its own representation in v2.

Cancer · v1 forks (treatment-burden)

v1 ships the cancer fork as a "cancer treatment burden" event. Cancer itself rarely drops VO₂max acutely; treatment is what drives capacity loss (chemo cardiotoxicity, radiation pulmonary effects, surgical recovery, deconditioning during treatment). Survivors carry 10-30% permanent VO₂max reductions per Jones 2012, anchored in Decision 4's recovery parameters. The event is labeled "cancer treatment" rather than "cancer" so the visible mechanism on the chart matches the literature.

Chronic kidney disease · v2 (slope modifier with dialysis step-down)

CKD does not fit the discrete-event model. eGFR descent through stages is gradual; CKD lowers VO₂max independently through uremic myopathy, anemia, and fluid status. v2 representation: a slope modifier proportional to CKD stage, with a step-down event at dialysis initiation (~30% acute drop). v1 does not render CKD because the slope-modifier construction is incompatible with the joint-outcome fork architecture — it's a separate representation that needs its own decision log when it ships.

Metabolic syndrome · folded into Underlying Risk (no separate visual)

Metabolic syndrome is a state, not a thing that happens. It elevates CV event probability, accelerates kidney decline, and increases cancer incidence. Modeling it as discrete events would misrepresent the mechanism. Instead, it is folded into Underlying Risk: higher metabolic burden contributes to a higher Underlying Risk slider position, which raises R_eff, which raises CV and cancer probabilities. This stance is permanent — not a v2 deferral. Giving metabolic its own slope penalty would double-count pathways already represented through CV and cancer event probabilities.

Neurodegenerative · v1 dashed-curve visual signature

Neurodegenerative disease (dementia, Parkinson's) presents the hardest case. The y-axis decision explicitly excluded cognitive decline. But excluding the domain entirely understates its impact on lived outcomes. v1 ships a dashed-segment treatment: at an R_eff-dependent age (84 at low risk, 73 at high risk; not rendered for very low risk), the trajectory transitions from solid to dashed. This signals "this curve no longer represents the full trajectory; cognitive and ADL decline are happening on a separate axis the tool does not render." A physician sees the dashed segment and immediately understands what the tool is and is not claiming. The dashed signal is the teaching — the tool visibly reaches its limit, and that limit is a feature, not a defect.

Hip fracture, stroke (separate from CV composite), HF onset, dialysis · v2 forks

Each of these is a defensible v2 fork with its own modal age, probability anchors, and recovery dynamics already partially specified in Decision 4. They are deferred from v1 only for visual-saturation reasons (each additional fork doubles the joint outcome count, and four forks means sixteen lines on the chart). v2 introduces them alongside the per-domain Underlying Risk decomposition, which will let the user dial up domain-specific risk and see only the relevant forks for that profile.

Decision 4 · Recovery dynamics parameterization

Choice: Each event class is parameterized by three numbers: acute drop magnitude (% of pre-event VO₂max), recovery time constant (months to plateau), and asymptotic recovery fraction (% of acute drop recovered at plateau).

Reasoning: Three parameters per event class is the minimum required to represent the shape of recovery (drop, return-to-asymptote, residual penalty) without overfitting. Calibration targets exist in the literature for each event class:

Stacking semantics (v1 implementation): within the "both" branch of the joint-outcome model (per Decision 2), event factors compound multiplicatively, not additively. Each event's acute drop is a fraction of capacity at the time the event fires, not an absolute mL/kg/min subtraction against the no-event baseline. With both events fired, capacity equals base capacity × (cancer event factor) × (CV event factor) at every age past the later event. This preserves the teaching payload that Conditioning still buys altitude even when both events hit: an Elite/HighR profile lands meaningfully higher than a Decon/HighR profile in late life because the same fractional drops apply to a higher starting capacity. An additive-stacking implementation would floor both profiles to roughly the same late-life value and erase the Conditioning advantage, which is biologically wrong (the literature supports a multiplicative model).

Decision 5 · Conditioning ↔ Lifestyle Optimization independence

Choice: Declared orthogonal by construction. Cross-talk disclosed in the panel covered by Decision 1.

Reasoning: Already covered above. The two levers are physiologically coupled (sleep affects training capacity, training affects sleep quality, hormone optimization affects both, etc.). The teaching tool benefits from independence. The disclosure handles the honesty cost.

Architectural decision · Single Underlying Risk slider

Choice: Underlying Risk remains a single aggregate slider. Per-domain risks (CV, kidney, cancer, neurodegenerative) are not exposed as separate sliders.

Reasoning: Fragmenting the slider into four or five domain-specific sliders is more physiologically honest — a real person can have high CV risk and low cancer risk — but it breaks the three-lever architecture and turns the tool into a personalized risk calculator. The tool's purpose is teaching, not personalized prediction. The single slider value combines with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization to produce R_eff; R_eff drives both lifetime probability and modal age for each forking event per Decision 2. Disclosure: "Real risk profiles are domain-specific. The slider represents aggregate underlying risk for teaching purposes."

What this is not: a claim that a real person's CV and cancer risks are correlated at this level. They are not, in many cases. The simplification is acknowledged as a teaching simplification, not a clinical claim.

What is now locked, and what is still open

Locked for v1: y-axis (VO₂max FRIEND-normed); three-lever architecture (Conditioning, Lifestyle Optimization, Underlying Risk); R_eff combination model with the 0.55 / 0.35 / 0.10 weights; joint-outcome divergent timelines with two forking events (cancer treatment, CV event) generating four branches; R_eff drives both lifetime probability and modal age for each event; multiplicative event stacking on capacity; per-branch vitality render with permanent residual matching the capacity residual; neurodegenerative dashed-segment visual past R_eff-dependent age; single Underlying Risk slider; metabolic syndrome folded permanently into Underlying Risk (not deferred).

Still open inside v1 (deferred to implementation): very-old tail (90+) uncertainty rendering; cross-talk panel UI placement and copy; color ramp accessibility audit (red-green color-blind safety vs alternate ramp).

Curve construction: choice and rationale (decision log)

IN BRIEF The curve is built by interpolating between a small library of empirical reference trajectories, not by computing from a universal formula. Conditioning selects which trajectory family the curve belongs to (elite, average, deconditioned). Age decay within each family follows published cohort shapes (BLSA, masters athletes, NHANES, HRS). Lifestyle Optimization applies a small slope multiplier on top. Underlying Risk overlays events at modal ages with calibrated drop and recovery dynamics. The curve is reproducible: same slider positions always produce the same curve. This construction matches the "directionally honest, not patient-fidelity" precision target locked in Scope and Audience.

Why this decision matters

Curve construction is what turns three slider positions into a rendered trajectory. The choice between formula-based, template-based, and hybrid construction propagates everywhere: it determines what calibration data is needed, how the curve responds to slider changes, whether two viewers see the same curve for the same inputs, and how honest the tool can claim to be. Choosing wrong here means either over-promising precision the tool cannot deliver, or under-delivering visual coherence the tool needs to teach.

Three candidate approaches

Option A · Universal formula with parameters

One parametric curve (e.g., logistic or Gompertz) with floor, slope, and inflection point modulated by Conditioning and Underlying Risk. Lifestyle Optimization tweaks slope. Events overlay drops.

Pro: Smooth slider response. Mathematically tidy. Easy to implement.

Con: One curve family cannot represent all profiles. Real trajectory shapes differ between elite-trained adults (long high plateau, late steep compression), average adults (gradual decline accelerating after 75), and deconditioned high-risk adults (lower starting capacity, earlier decline, no plateau). A universal formula forces all three into the same family with different parameters, which produces visually plausible corners but implausible interpolations between them. The prior version of this prototype made exactly this mistake.

Option B · Empirical reference trajectories with interpolation

Build a small library of reference trajectories, each calibrated against a published cohort or literature family. Conditioning selects which trajectories to interpolate between. Age decay within each trajectory follows that cohort's shape. Slider positions interpolate between named templates rather than tuning one universal formula.

Pro: Honest about the fact that different lever profiles produce genuinely different curve shapes. Each template can be defended by name against a specific cohort or literature family. Avoids the "over-tuned formula" trap. Matches what aging research actually does.

Con: Library construction takes work. Interpolation between templates needs to be visually smooth or the curve will jump as sliders move. Requires deciding how many templates is enough.

Option C · Hybrid — formula for trajectory shape, templates for parameter ranges

One parametric curve, but its parameters are pulled from cohort-derived ranges rather than tuned by hand. Conditioning maps to BLSA-derived slope coefficients. Underlying Risk maps to event probabilities and ages from PREVENT/FRAX/SEER. The formula is the rendering layer; the cohorts are the calibration layer.

Pro: Smooth response of A with the cohort-anchored honesty of B. Implementable without a full template library.

Con: Still inherits A's core problem of forcing all profiles into one curve family. Cohort-derived parameters do not solve the fact that elite trajectories and deconditioned trajectories are different shapes, not just different parameters.

Choice: Option B with three reference trajectories at v1, expandable

The teaching tool's purpose is for viewers to internalize the structural difference between conditioning families. That difference is the difference in trajectory shape, not just trajectory position. A formula collapses shape difference into parameter difference and loses the teaching payload. Templates preserve it. The audience precision target ("directionally honest, internalize patterns") is exactly served by named, defensible templates.

What about Well-conditioned (between Elite and Average)? Interpolated. The Conditioning slider's intermediate positions blend the Elite and Average templates by weighted average. Same for "between Average and Deconditioned." This is the interpolation work the templates buy.

How the levers attach to the templates

Conditioning

Selects which two templates to interpolate between, and the weight. Elite slider position = pure Elite template. Deconditioned position = pure Deconditioned template. Anywhere in between = weighted blend. This is the dominant lever and produces the largest visible curve change.

Lifestyle Optimization (slope effect)

Applies a multiplier on the rate of decline within whatever blended template Conditioning selected. Highly Optimized = decline rate × 0.9 (10% slower). Maintained = no change. Neglected = decline rate × 1.1 (10% faster). The ±10% bound matches the defensible range stated in the lever architecture section. Lifestyle does not shift the starting altitude — that is Conditioning's job. Implementation: scale the cumulative drop from the age-30 baseline by the multiplier at every age, so capacity(age) = baseline + mult · (template(age) − baseline).

Lifestyle Optimization (vitality render)

Drives the line color via the render mix formula in the Vitality decision log. This is Lifestyle Optimization's primary mechanism and visible signature.

Underlying Risk

Combined with Conditioning and Lifestyle Optimization into R_eff. R_eff drives both lifetime probability and modal age for each forking event per Modeling Decision 2. Each event renders as an acute multiplicative drop with recovery curve following the parameters in Modeling Decision 4. The events sit on top of the trajectory; they do not change the underlying template.

Age

The x-axis. Trajectory follows the template's published shape from age 30 to 90 solid, age 90 to 100 dashed (uncertainty rendering, deferred from y-axis open questions).

What about events that fire on a template that doesn't normally produce them

An Elite-conditioned, High-UR profile still produces forks at the modal ages and probabilities computed from R_eff. The Elite + High Underlying Risk combination yields a moderate R_eff because Conditioning's protective contribution offsets some of the inherited loading — events still fork, just later and with lower probability than a Deconditioned + High Underlying Risk profile at the same Underlying Risk slider position. On the with-event branches, capacity drops multiplicatively per Decision 4's recovery parameters; the post-event trajectory continues to follow the Elite template's slope from the recovered point. Conditioning does not become "less Elite" because of the event — the slider remains where the user set it. The cross-talk panel discloses that real events do change conditioning, and the tool does not model this.

Reproducibility

Same slider positions always produce the same curve. No stochastic resampling. This is locked from Modeling Decision 2 and reaffirmed here because curve construction is where reproducibility either holds or breaks. The viewer can compare two slider configurations directly because the only thing changing between them is the lever positions.

What this construction is honest about, and what it admits

Honest: The three templates are real published cohort shapes. The interpolation between them is mathematically simple but visually defensible. The events are real published modal ages and recovery trajectories. The vitality render is anchored to validated PROs. Every visible feature of the curve has a defensible source.

Admits: Real individual trajectories vary far more than three templates can capture. Two patients with the same Conditioning slider position will have different curves in real life because they have different bodies, behaviors, genetics, and histories. The tool teaches the structure of the levers; it does not predict any individual person's path. This admission is not buried — it is the point of the Scope and Audience section.

v2 expansion targets

  1. More templates. Add sex-specific Elite/Average/Deconditioned. Add age-of-onset variations for events. Add cancer-survivor and post-cardiac-event templates as their own trajectory families.
  2. Smoother interpolation. v1 uses linear blending between templates. v2 may use spline interpolation if the linear blend produces visually rough transitions.
  3. Cohort-fit rather than literature-anchored templates. v1 templates are constructed from published cohort means and percentile tables. v2 fits trajectory shapes directly from longitudinal data (BLSA, HRS, UK Biobank).
  4. Empirical Lifestyle slope effect. v1 uses a clinical-prior ±10% multiplier. v2 fits the slope effect from longitudinal data stratified by Lifestyle Optimization PRO scores.

Open questions deferred to implementation

  1. Linear vs spline interpolation between templates. Likely linear in v1, evaluate visually.
  2. How to handle the very-old tail (90 to 100) where masters-athlete data thins. Likely render dashed, anchored to centenarian disability literature.
  3. Whether sex-specific templates ship in v1 or wait for v2. Argues for sex-aware percentile lookup but shared trajectory shapes in v1.
  4. Whether the user can see the templates labeled on the chart (e.g., a faint "Average" reference curve always visible) or only the active interpolated curve. Likely the active curve only, with optional reference toggle.
